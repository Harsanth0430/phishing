How to recognize phishing emails & fake websites

Sender & Header

Mismatch alert: The display name says “Bank Support” but the address is bank.support@gmail.com.

  • Hover to reveal full email address
  • Look for misspelled domains (e.g., paypaI.com with capital i)
  • Beware of “no-reply” used to block replies

Links & URLs

Always hover before you click. Check if the link points to the real domain.

  • Use HTTPS (lock icon) but don’t trust it alone
  • Watch for subdomain tricks: bank.com.example.co
  • Short links? Expand with a trusted expander first

Language & Tone

Urgency, threats, or gifts push you to act fast.

  • “Your account will be closed in 1 hour”
  • “Confirm to claim a reward”
  • Typos and generic greetings (e.g., “Dear user”)

Attachments

Unexpected invoices, resumes, or password-protected files are classic traps.

  • Beware of .html, .exe, .js, or macro-enabled docs
  • Scan files with a reputable scanner before opening

Website Footprints

Fake pages often miss fundamentals.

  • Broken footer links / no privacy policy
  • Low-quality logos, stock images, or odd spacing
  • Login page appears before the main site

Technical Checks

For advanced users:

  • Check domain age with a WHOIS lookup
  • Use password managers: they won’t auto-fill on fake domains
  • View message headers for SPF/DKIM/DMARC results

Social engineering tactics

Baiting

Enticing offers (“free gift cards”) that lure clicks or downloads.

Pretexting

Fake scenarios (IT support, HR, bank) to make you share data.

Quid Pro Quo

“Do this & get that” exchanges that request credentials or access.

Tailgating

Following someone into a restricted space without a badge.

Spear Phishing

Personalized attacks using info from social media or leaks.

Vishing/Smishing

Voice/SMS phishing: urgent calls or texts with malicious links.

Best practices to stay safe

Do

  • Verify sender identity via official channels
  • Use MFA (authenticator app or hardware key)
  • Report suspicious messages to your security team
  • Type URLs manually for sensitive logins
  • Keep software and browsers up to date

Don’t

  • Reuse passwords across sites
  • Share OTPs, recovery codes, or MFA prompts
  • Download unexpected attachments
  • Plug unknown USB drives into work devices
  • Ignore browser/security warnings
Tip: Password managers help detect fake sites by refusing to auto-fill on the wrong domain.

Real-world style examples

“Bank account locked” email

Red flags: generic greeting, urgent deadline, link to bank-secure-login.example.co.

What to do: Don’t click. Visit your bank by typing the URL or using the official app.

“Invoice attached” from an unknown vendor

Attachment is Invoice_Aug.html (opens a fake login). Always verify with the requester first.

“CEO urgent gift cards” message

Common business email compromise ploy. Verify via a known phone number before acting.
